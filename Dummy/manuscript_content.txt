Which Genetic Algorithm Operator Combination Best Fits the Vehicle Routing Problem? A Comprehensive Study on Representation, Selection, Crossover, and Mutation
Abstract
The Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) remains one of the most challenging combinatorial optimization problems in operations research, with significant practical applications in logistics and transportation. Genetic Algorithms (GAs) have emerged as powerful metaheuristic approaches for solving VRP, but the selection of appropriate GA operators significantly impacts solution quality and computational efficiency. This study presents a comprehensive comparison of GA operators, systematically evaluating 354 different method combinations across representation schemes, selection methods, crossover operators, and mutation strategies. Using the well-established Solomon benchmark datasets comprising 56 problem instances across clustered (C), random (R), and random-clustered (RC) configurations, we conducted extensive experiments with rigorous statistical validation. Our results demonstrate that permutation-based representation combined with tournament selection, Order Crossover (OX), and swap mutation achieves superior performance across most problem types, with an average improvement of 15-20% over baseline methods. The study provides detailed performance metrics including solution quality, convergence speed, computational efficiency, and population diversity analysis. These findings offer practical guidelines for practitioners implementing GAs for VRP and contribute to the theoretical understanding of operator interactions in evolutionary computation for routing problems.
Keywords: Genetic Algorithm, Vehicle Routing Problem, Operator Comparison, Solomon Benchmark, Evolutionary Computation, Metaheuristics, Optimization
1. Introduction
The Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) is a classical combinatorial optimization problem in operations research with the goal of designing a set of minimum‑cost routes for a fleet of vehicles to serve geographically distributed customers, subject to constraints such as vehicle capacity, time windows, and route length (Schneider, 2022). VRP is known to be NP‑hard, with the search space growing factorially with the number of customers; for example, a 100-customer instance has approximately 10157 possible solutions, making brute-force approaches infeasible. Innovations in metaheuristics, such as hybrid Tabu Search and clustering approaches, have led to improved solutions and significant reductions in computational time for VRP and its variants like capacitated VRP and VRP with time windows (Boussaid et al., 2020). For instance, metaheuristics have brought cost improvements and allowed instances featuring 50–100 customers to be solved in minutes instead of hours or days. These advancements translate into measurable savings in fuel consumption, fleet size, and service efficiency for logistics providers, which remains a key motivation for ongoing research (Fajrin).
Despite the availability of exact optimization methods such as branch‑and‑bound, branch‑and‑cut, and mixed‑integer linear programming, these approaches are generally limited to small or moderately sized VRP instances, because the underlying formulations exhibit exponential growth in the number of routes and constraints, leading to rapidly increasing memory requirements as problem size grows (Bogyrbayeva et al., 2022). In practice, exact solvers tend to be confined to benchmark or inventory‑routing variants with relatively few customers; for example, a recent branch‑and‑cut scheme for multi‑vehicle and multi‑depot inventory‑routing problems reports 417 proven optimal solutions over 638 small instances and can still prove optimality for all single‑vehicle instances with up to 50 customers, but for 100‑customer cases it delivers mainly best‑known rather than proven optimal solutions (Xxxxx). This behavior illustrates how, for larger and more realistic VRP or VRPTW instances, the size of the branch‑and‑bound tree and the time needed to close even small optimality gaps often become prohibitive, which motivates the use of approximate heuristics and metaheuristics that trade guaranteed optimality for high‑quality solutions obtained within acceptable time limits (Bogyrbayeva et al., 2022). Empirical studies on time‑dependent VRPs with time windows using Solomon’s 56 benchmark instances with 100 customer nodes similarly favor metaheuristics such as genetic algorithms, showing that exact MILP‑based methods struggle to reach optimality in reasonable CPU times once tight time‑window or additional real‑world constraints are included (Xxxxx, 2015).​
To address these limitations, researchers and practitioners have extensively adopted heuristic and metaheuristic algorithms, including constructive heuristics such as Clarke–Wright savings and sweep algorithms, local‑search–based improvements like 2‑opt, 3‑opt, and Or‑opt, and more advanced metaheuristics such as Tabu Search, Simulated Annealing, Ant Colony Optimization, and Genetic Algorithms (GAs) (xxxxx). These approaches can efficiently explore large and structured search spaces; for example, a Hybrid Genetic Algorithm with Solomon’s insertion heuristic for the VRPTW attains best‑known solutions on 11 classical Solomon benchmark instances and even improves the best‑known solution in 1 instance, while keeping deviations from best‑known values small and computation times practical (Rahman et al., 2024). Similarly, an improved GA for vehicle path optimization has been shown to increase average convergence speed by 50–60% and reduce computation time by about 48% compared with a classical GA, while maintaining solution accuracy within about 1% of the reference solution, illustrating the ability of metaheuristics to deliver high‑quality solutions rapidly (Li et al., 2024). Empirical studies and surveys on classic benchmark suites, including Solomon and Cordeau instances, consistently report that well‑designed metaheuristics can close most of the optimality gap and often match or come very close to best‑known solutions for many VRP variants, while remaining flexible enough to incorporate complex, problem‑specific constraints (Benedyk et al., 2023)
Genetic Algorithms (GAs) have been widely explored for VRP since the 1990s because their population‑based search makes it easy to encode routes as chromosomes and to exploit problem structure via tailored selection schemes, crossover operators, mutation strategies, and hybrid local search, as documented in recent surveys and GA‑focused studies on VRP (xxxxx). Many computational studies report that well‑designed GA‑based methods can match or surpass traditional heuristics, especially when hybridized with local search or ruin‑and‑recreate phases: for example, a two‑phase distributed ruin‑and‑recreate GA for VRPTW produces solutions on Solomon benchmarks that are superior to several recently published hybrid algorithms and even to some best‑known solutions, while a hybrid Genetic–Simulated Annealing algorithm for CVRP tested on 86 benchmark instances yields high‑quality routes with reduced travel distance compared to baseline methods (xxxxxx ). Similarly, adaptive or specialized GAs for rich VRP variants with two‑dimensional loading and heterogeneous fleets have been validated on up to 150 benchmark instances and on all available datasets for specific problem types, consistently showing competitive or improved solution quality and cost savings relative to earlier heuristics (xxxxxx). At the same time, empirical analyses demonstrate that GA performance is highly sensitive to design decisions: studies that systematically compare different route encodings show substantial impacts on population diversity and convergence behavior, while work on memetic and improved GA variants highlights how tuning selection pressure and operator design is crucial for balancing exploration and exploitation in large‑scale VRPTW (xxxxxx). Moreover, investigations of enhanced mutation and repair strategies in vehicle path and routing contexts report that improved GAs can increase average convergence speed by about 50–60% and cut computation time by roughly 48% while keeping solution error within about 1% of reference solutions, underscoring how crossover, mutation, and repair operators that preserve promising route segments and feasibility have a decisive influence on GA effectiveness (xxxxxxx ).
A well‑known drawback of Genetic Algorithms (GAs) in VRP and related routing applications is their tendency to converge prematurely and become trapped in local optima, with studies on CVRP, VRPTW, and rich VRPs explicitly noting early stagnation, infeasible solutions, or low‑quality routes when standard GA designs are used (xxxx). This behavior is closely linked to loss of population diversity, which motivates mechanisms such as multi‑population structures, solution archives, and niche or migration strategies: for example, a multi‑population GA for rich VRPs evolves several subpopulations that share solutions to delay premature convergence, while an improved GA–ACO for VRPTW uses two interacting populations plus Generalized Variable Neighborhood Search to avoid local optima, matching best‑known solutions in Solomon Class C and reducing vehicle usage in Class R by 24.45% with a distance deviation of 9.19% (xxxxx). Diversity‑preservation and escape mechanisms such as tailored mutation, elite‑but‑diverse selection, and parallel multi‑process or archive‑based schemes are central design levers in these works, and empirical results show that tuned operators can cut CO₂ emissions by up to 4% and outperform traditional GA, ant colony, and bee colony approaches on standard CVRP benchmarks (xxxxx). From a theoretical standpoint, the no‑free‑lunch theorem for optimization states that, averaged over all objective functions on a fixed search space, no algorithm (and hence no GA configuration) is universally superior, which directly implies that GA effectiveness for VRP depends on matching representation, selection pressure, and variation operators to the structure of the specific routing class under study (Wolpert and Macready, 1997). Consequently, recent GA‑for‑VRP research increasingly emphasizes systematic empirical studies and controlled ablation of operator combinations—such as comparing different initialisation heuristics, crossover operators, and embedded local searches on suites of 20–50 or more benchmark instances—rather than relying on ad‑hoc parameter choices, in order to understand and mitigate premature convergence in large, rugged VRP search spaces (xxxxxx).​
This study systematically evaluates 354 combinations of genetic algorithm operators—including representation schemes (binary, real‑valued, permutation), selection methods (tournament, roulette wheel, rank‑based, SUS, elitism, Boltzmann, stairwise), crossover operators (single‑point, two‑point, uniform, SBX, BLX‑α, PMX, OX, CX, ERX, SCX), and mutation strategies (swap, insert, inversion, scramble, bit‑flip, Gaussian, polynomial)—for solving the Vehicle Routing Problem using the Solomon benchmark datasets comprising 56 VRPTW instances with 100 customers each. The research provides a comprehensive operator‑level comparison within a standardized experimental framework, including 99,120 independent GA runs (354 combinations × 56 instances × 5 seeds), detailed performance metrics on solution quality, convergence behavior, and computational effort, and rigorous statistical analysis using confidence intervals, non‑parametric tests, and effect sizes to identify statistically and practically superior operator combinations. The results yield insights into interaction effects between representation, selection, crossover, and mutation, highlight robust operator configurations such as permutation‑based encodings with tournament selection and OX‑type crossovers, and distill practical implementation guidelines supported by open‑source code and statistically validated evidence for practitioners deploying GAs to solve VRP in diverse settings.​
2. Literature Review
2.1 Vehicle Routing Problem
The Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) is commonly defined as an NP-hard generalization of the Traveling Salesman Problem in which a fleet of vehicles must start and end at one or more depots while visiting all customers on a graph so that total routing cost (distance, time, or other metrics) is minimized (Bogyrbayeva et al., 2022; Vidal et al., 2020). In real logistics systems this optimization is economically critical, with U.S. firms reported to spend about 10.4% of their revenue on transportation, logistics costs accounting for roughly 8% of GDP, and last‑mile distribution alone representing around 50% of total transportation costs, which explains the intense interest in better VRP algorithms (Bogyrbayeva et al., 2022). Core variants such as the Capacitated VRP (CVRP) and VRP with Time Windows (VRPTW) constrain vehicle loads and specify customer service intervals while preserving the same NP-hard combinatorial structure, and recent surveys categorize these within a broader family of “rich” VRPs that incorporate many realistic constraints (Mor & Speranza, 2020). Further extensions introduce additional structural complexity, including multi-commodity flows (Gu, 2024), multi-trip and multi-depot routing with time windows and release dates (Zhen et al., 2020), and time-evolving or periodic routing decisions that link successive planning horizons (Mor & Speranza, 2020). Recent work also addresses pickup-and-delivery services, heterogeneous fleets, split deliveries, and uncertainty: for example, an exact branch-and-cut method complemented by an adaptive large neighborhood search heuristic for the split-delivery VRP achieves average gaps of about 0.19–0.18% from best-known solutions and an overall optimality gap near 1.82% on benchmark instances, while stochastic two-echelon inventory-routing and medical-supply applications illustrate how random demand and multi-level networks can be embedded into routing formulations (Rave et al., 2025). Because the feasible solution space grows combinatorially with the number of customers, these studies consistently report that exact mixed-integer optimization can handle only small or medium instances, so modern research relies heavily on advanced heuristics, metaheuristics, and increasingly machine-learning-based methods to obtain near-optimal VRP solutions at practical scales (Bogyrbayeva et al., 2022).​
Researchers now typically distinguish two main classes of algorithms for the VRP: exact methods, which aim at proven optimality, and heuristic/metaheuristic methods, which trade guarantees for scalability (Liu et al., 2023). Exact algorithms combine techniques such as branch-and-bound, branch-and-cut, and column generation in modern branch-price-and-cut or branch-cut-and-price frameworks, and recent unified exact approaches can optimally solve a broad family of VRP variants but are still limited to instances with at most a few dozen customers per route pattern—for example, a state-of-the-art column-generation–based method for two-dimensional or two-level CVRP solves instances with up to about 35 customers to proven optimality (Zhang et al., 2022). Because of these size limits, many practical studies rely on classical constructive heuristics such as the savings algorithm of Clarke and Wright, sweep-based clustering, or nearest-neighbor rules, followed by local-improvement moves like 2‑opt, 3‑opt, and Or‑opt, which are routinely used as building blocks in contemporary VRP heuristics and surveys (Liu et al., 2023). More advanced metaheuristics—including Tabu Search, Simulated Annealing, Genetic Algorithms, Ant Colony Optimization, and variants like Adaptive Variable Neighborhood Search—have been shown to yield substantially higher-quality solutions on realistic instances, with case studies reporting, for example, that metaheuristic hybrids for a capacitated VRP with university shuttle routes outperform simpler heuristics when routing four vehicles, and that an improved ACO for VRPTW reduces total distance to about 391.1 km compared with roughly 403.1 km for a classical ACO and 391 km for a Tabu Search baseline (Ibrahim & Ishaya, 2022). In applied logistics settings such as medical waste collection, guidance-based local search and metaheuristics have also demonstrated around 20–30% reductions in total travel time relative to initial heuristic routes, illustrating why metaheuristics are now considered the dominant practical approach for large and complex VRP variants (Liu et al., 2023).​
Benchmark datasets are now a standard tool for evaluating VRP algorithms, with recent studies repeatedly confirming that performance comparisons almost always rely on a small set of well‑known benchmark families (Meira et al., 2020). Solomon’s VRPTW benchmark remains the primary reference for time‑window problems; it consists of 56 instances with 100 customers partitioned into six classes (R1, C1, RC1, R2, C2, RC2), which correspond to random, clustered, and mixed customer distributions with short or long planning horizons, and these instances are still used in modern metaheuristic experiments where, for example, a hybrid swarm‑intelligence method reaches the optimal solution in 42 of the 56 cases (Shen et al., 2020). Building on this, the extended Gehring and Homberger VRPTW set provides larger instances with 200, 400, 600, 800, and 1 000 customers, giving 1000‑customer test cases that are widely used to stress‑test advanced local search and learning‑based solvers (Gehring & Homberger, 1999). For capacitated vehicle routing, the Golden benchmark contributes 20 classical CVRP instances with 240–483 customers, while more recent work by Uchoa et al. consolidated and extended these into the CVRPLIB “X‑instances,” a set of 100 instances ranging from 100 to 1 000 customers with systematically varied depot position, customer distribution (random, clustered, random‑clustered), demand pattern, and average route length. Together with Cordeau‑type multi‑depot and rich VRP benchmarks summarized in recent surveys, these suites provide a diverse collection of medium to very large scale instances that underpin the empirical assessment and comparison of modern exact, heuristic, and learning‑based VRP algorithms (Meira et al., 2020).​
​2.2 Genetic Algorithms
Genetic Algorithms (GAs) are adaptive population-based search procedures inspired by natural selection and genetics, where a set of candidate solutions (chromosomes) evolves over time toward regions of higher fitness through selection, crossover, and mutation operators (Holland, 1975). In the standard GA cycle, an initial population is randomly generated, each individual is evaluated by a fitness function, fitter individuals are selected as parents, crossover recombines their encoded features to create offspring, mutation introduces random perturbations to maintain diversity, the offspring are evaluated, and a replacement strategy (generational or steady-state) forms the next population until a termination criterion such as a maximum number of generations or convergence is reached (Goldberg, 1989). Core GA components include the encoding that maps problem solutions to chromosomes, a population that enables parallel exploration of the search space, a fitness function that drives the search, selection mechanisms that control selection pressure, genetic operators (crossover and mutation) that balance exploitation and exploration, and a replacement policy that determines how new individuals enter and old ones leave the population (Siagian & Lestari, 2022). The theoretical foundations of GAs rest on Holland’s building block hypothesis, which argues that GAs work by identifying and recombining short, high-quality schemata (building blocks) into increasingly fit solutions (Holland, 1975), and on the no free lunch theorem, which shows that no single optimization algorithm is universally best over all possible problems, thereby motivating problem-specific choices of representations, operators, and parameter settings for GAs (Wolpert & Macready, 1997).​
Fig. 1 Genetic Algorithm
The basic GA process follows this cycle: (1) Initialize a random population, (2) Evaluate fitness of each individual, (3) Select parents based on fitness, (4) Apply crossover to create offspring, (5) Apply mutation to offspring, (6) Evaluate offspring fitness, (7) Form new generation through replacement strategy, (8) Repeat until termination criteria met.
Recent evolutionary computation studies reinterpret Holland’s building block hypothesis by showing that GA exploit recurring high‑fitness gene patterns, often called dominant or key blocks, which can be explicitly mined and recombined to accelerate convergence; for instance, a 2025 hybrid GA for reconfigurable manufacturing layouts that extracts “dominant blocks” via association rules reduces the average number of iterations from 121 for a baseline GA to 50 and cuts computation time from about 8.9 s to 5.3 s while maintaining better fitness values on a 10‑machine, 3‑period layout problem (Wei et al., 2025). Building on this view, recent reviews of the no free lunch theorem for optimization emphasize that when performance is averaged over all possible objective functions no algorithm, including GAs, can outperform all others, so any gains necessarily come from exploiting structure in restricted problem classes, which motivates tailoring encodings, selection pressure, and operators to the domain at hand (Oltean, 2021; Papadopoulos, 2024; Wolpert & Macready, 1997). Modern GA surveys and monographs describe a standard component set: an encoding that maps problem solutions to chromosomes; a population of candidate solutions that supports parallel exploration; a fitness function that quantifies solution quality; selection mechanisms that regulate selection pressure to balance exploitation of good individuals and exploration of new regions; crossover and mutation operators that recombine and perturb chromosomes to generate diversity; and a replacement strategy—often generational or steady‑state—that decides how offspring enter the population and low‑quality individuals are discarded, with empirical studies on feature selection and engineering design reporting that carefully tuned combinations of these elements yield consistent improvements in accuracy and convergence speed over untuned “vanilla” GAs across dozens of benchmark problems (Aldamani et al., 2025; Kordon et al., 2023).​
2.3 Representation Schemes for VRP
Encoding (representation) in a Genetic Algorithm for VRP determines how routes are mapped to chromosomes, which moves are reachable by crossover and mutation, and how easily feasibility (capacity, time windows, number of vehicles) can be preserved, so it has a direct impact on both solution quality and runtime. Recent VRP studies show that different encodings can lead to markedly different outcomes on the same instance: permutation-based GAs that encode customer sequences tend to work very well when local search moves (2‑opt, relocate, swap) are integrated, as in a guided GA for UAV-based CVRP that clearly outperforms a baseline GA in both route cost and convergence speed (Jasim & Ahmed, 2024), and a hybrid PSO–GA for LPG distribution that reports up to 4.56% transportation cost reduction versus current company routes on real data (Indrianti et al., 2025). In contrast, random‑key (real‑valued) encodings separate the GA search in [0,1]n from a problem‑specific decoder, allowing a single representation and operator set to handle open VRP, multi-depot VRP, and dial‑a‑ride variants while consistently producing near best-known solutions on standard benchmarks (Kummer & Nascimento, 2020; Londe et al., 2025; Shimaoka et al., 2025). Binary or matrix-based encodings remain useful when decisions are inherently yes/no—such as time-window assignment or activation of links—and are frequently used in multiobjective formulations where GA must handle both routing and scheduling or service-level decisions; for example, recent network and path-optimization applications show that carefully tuned binary or discrete encodings combined with improved mutation can significantly outperform classical GA and particle swarm variants in terms of total travel cost and convergence behaviour (Bhattacharjya, 2020; Sitorus et al., 2022; Mu et al., 2024). Overall, empirical comparisons in recent reviews emphasize that no single encoding is universally best: permutation representations are usually preferred for pure routing structure, random‑key encodings for hybrid or hierarchical VRP/location problems, and binary/matrix encodings for problems with many side constraints or assignment decisions, reinforcing that representation must be chosen to match instance characteristics and algorithm design (Vie et al., 2020; Londe et al., 2025).

2.4 Selection Methods
Selection in Genetic Algorithms (GAs) controls which individuals reproduce and therefore how strongly the search exploits high‑fitness solutions versus exploring new regions, making it a critical design choice for VRP GAs where small differences in selection pressure can translate into several percent differences in route cost or convergence time. Recent hyperparameter studies show that combining strong selection with elitism can dramatically improve performance: for example, Hutchison et al. (2023) found that a GA using three‑way tournament selection and 50% elitism achieved the best balance of finding the global optimum (“champion”), covering about 42% of the top candidates, and achieving large speedups (≈20×) over brute force in a 100 128‑candidate molecular design problem. Brouwer et al. (2022) reported that tournament‑based survivor selection outperformed the default elitist “plus‑selection” method on 83–88% of 59 benchmark functions, with elitist tournament alone accounting for about 54% of all best results, while roulette‑wheel variants performed close to chance on the same suite. At the same time, comparative work in spatial layout optimization by Ibrahim et al. (2025) showed that roulette wheel selection outperformed rank selection in 50% of experiments and reduced processing time by up to 38% in some runs, although rank selection occasionally converged faster in specific scenarios, illustrating how problem structure and implementation details can reverse preferences between methods. Additional studies comparing Boltzmann-style or fitness-uniform schemes against classical proportional selection emphasize that diversity-preserving schemes can delay premature convergence but may converge more slowly, so practical GA designs for VRP often combine tournament or roulette selection with rank-based or temperature-controlled variants plus elitism to guarantee non‑decreasing best fitness over generations (Brouwer et al., 2022; Ibrahim et al., 2025; Jebari & Madiafi, 2013; McCall, 2010).​

2.5 Crossover Operators
Careful design of crossover operators is crucial in GA-based VRP because crossover largely dictates how effectively good route segments are recombined and how quickly the search explores new regions of the solution space. Empirical studies show that, for permutation encodings used in routing, the choice of crossover can change both best-found distance and runtime noticeably: for example, experiments on distance‑constrained VRP and multiple TSP instances report that sequential constructive crossover (SCX) obtains the best total distance in a clear majority of test cases and achieves the best maximum-vehicle distance in about 44% of instances, outperforming PMX, CX, ERX, and alternating-edge crossover in most comparisons (Ahmed, 2020; Al‑Furhud & Jaradat, 2020). Comparisons of order-based operators such as OX, PMX, and a modified CX2 on VRP benchmarks further indicate that CX2 attains the best fitness but is computationally expensive, PMX offers a good compromise between solution quality and runtime, and OX, while slightly weaker in fitness, explores a broader region of the search space and is therefore attractive when embedded in metaheuristics (Wibisono, 2021). For real‑valued encodings, simulated binary crossover (SBX) remains the de facto standard, and a recent self‑adaptive SBX–elitism scheme reduced average relative error by 99.99% and cut computation time by about 19.40% compared with a baseline self‑adaptive SBX on 10‑ to 30‑dimensional benchmarks (Novianti et al., 2024), while new operators such as Lagrange‑based LPX have been shown to outperform classic SBX and BLX‑α on many continuous test functions (Aladdin, 2023). Binary and mixed encodings, common in network and control applications, also show strong sensitivity to crossover choice: studies comparing single‑point, two‑point, multi‑point, and uniform crossover find that multi‑point and uniform crossovers can substantially change convergence speed and stability metrics, confirming that the operator must match both representation and problem structure (Güler & Yazici, 2025; Kurniawan et al., 2020). Overall, recent reviews emphasize that no single crossover is universally best; instead, order‑ and edge‑preserving operators (OX, PMX, ERX, SCX) tend to perform best for permutation‑based VRP, SBX and its variants for real‑coded hybrids, and multi‑point or uniform crossover for high‑dimensional binary encodings (Ahmed, 2022; Wibisono, 2021; Novianti et al., 2024).​

POS
Inherits specific positions from one parent and fills other positions based on the second parent’s order
Position-based scheme complementary to OBX; commonly used in experimental comparisons to study the effect of preserving absolute positions versus relative order in TSP/VRP encodings, with mixed results depending on problem structure (Li et al., 2022; Ahmed, 2022) 
ERX
Edge recombination that preserves adjacency (edges) from the parents
Designed to retain route structure by focusing on shared edges; CVRP and MTSP studies where ERX is compared to OX, PMX, CX, AEX, and SCX show that edge-focused operators often achieve competitive or superior route lengths when adjacency information is critical (Ahmed, 2022; Al‑Furhud & Jaradat, 2020)
SCX
Sequentially constructs an offspring route by choosing the next customer based on a distance or cost heuristic
Specifically tailored to routing; experiments on distance‑constrained VRP and MTSP report that SCX produces the best or tied‑best solutions in the majority of test cases and yields the best maximum-vehicle distance in about 44% of instances, outperforming PMX, CX, ERX, and AEX in both solution quality and robustness (Ahmed, 2020; Al‑Furhud & Jaradat, 2020; Al‑Baz & El‑Sawy, 2020) 
2.6 Mutation Operators
Mutation operators are a second, equally important design lever alongside crossover, because they control how new genetic material is injected into the search and how easily a GA can escape local optima under different encodings. For binary encodings, simple bit‑flip or random‑reset mutations are often sufficient and can be very powerful when combined with small populations: in a recent comparative study on the Max‑One problem with chromosome length 20, a new bit‑wise mutation variant converged in as few as 19 generations with zero failures across 100 runs, while swap and inverse mutations failed in 60 and 15 runs respectively and showed much higher median generations to convergence (378 vs. 40.5) [Ullah et al., 2022]. In contrast, real‑coded GAs for continuous multiobjective design problems typically rely on Gaussian, polynomial, or dynamic real‑valued mutation; for example, a dynamic mutation operator for real‑coded GA significantly improved convergence speed and solution quality compared with fixed Gaussian mutation on engineering benchmarks [Kim et al., 2024], while polynomial mutation combined with SBX is now standard in many multiobjective service composition and layout problems [Zhai et al., 2023]. Permutation encodings used in routing and scheduling require structure‑preserving operators (swap, insertion, inversion, scramble, displacement), and VRP/MTSP studies show that carefully tuned combinations of swap–insert–inversion mutations inside hybrid GAs can outperform state‑of‑the‑art metaheuristics on asymmetric distance‑constrained VRP instances and significantly reduce total distance and constraint violations in real pickup‑and‑delivery VRPTW case studies [Ahmed et al., 2022][Wicaksono et al., 2021][El‑Mekkawy et al., 2024]. Comprehensive mutation reviews further confirm that more disruptive operators such as scramble or displacement introduce diversity faster and are effective on large permutation problems, but they may temporarily deteriorate solution quality, whereas more local operators like swap or inversion produce smoother but slower improvements, so modern GA designs often mix several mutation types and tune rates according to encoding and problem scale [Srinivas & Patnaik, 2020][Ullah et al., 2022].​

Most existing GA-based VRP studies in Table X focus on tailoring operators within a single encoding and problem setting rather than systematically exploring the full operator design space across representations. For instance, the work on Stas crossover with K‑mean clustering for VRPTW uses a pure permutation encoding and mainly compares Stas against classical crossovers such as OX and PMX, while keeping selection and mutation fixed (Poohoi, Puntusavase, & Ohmori, 2024). Similarly, hybrid genetic algorithms for the asymmetric distance‑constrained VRP and improved GAs for VRPTW pick‑up and delivery adopt permutation-based solutions with carefully engineered crossover and route-improvement operators, but do not consider alternative encodings or families of selection schemes in a unified framework (Ahmed, Hameed, & Mutar, 2022; Indrianti, Pramudita, & co‑authors, 2021). Other routing variants such as the periodic heterogeneous VRP and the asymmetric capacitated VRP are addressed via single‑configuration permutation GAs that fix representation, selection, and most operators to one design, limiting comparability across operator choices (Khoidir & Garside, 2022; Meniz & Tiryaki, 2022).​.
Several applied and methodological papers do examine particular GA components, but always in isolation from a broad VRP operator study: improved GAs for generic VRP or VRPTW and GA-based path routing optimize specific crossover or parameter settings on limited datasets; real-world CVRPTW applications and urban terminal delivery models emphasize parameter tuning and fuzzy mutation; GA‑EM and related studies compare mutation operators on TSP or toy problems; and selection-operator decider frameworks analyse selection schemes in non‑VRP contexts (Zhu et al., 2025; Nowak et al., 2022; Rahman et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025; BinJubier et al., 2024; Ullah, Salam, & Masood, 2022; Brouwer, 2022). None of these studies jointly evaluates multiple representations (binary, real‑valued, permutation) together with a wide portfolio of selection, crossover, and mutation operators on the full Solomon VRPTW benchmark using large-scale, statistically rigorous experimentation. In contrast, the present work systematically assesses 354 operator combinations across all 56 Solomon 100‑customer instances (99,120 runs), quantifies performance with confidence intervals, non‑parametric tests, and effect sizes, and explicitly analyses interaction effects between representation, selection, crossover, and mutation to derive robust, evidence‑based GA design guidelines for VRP.​
